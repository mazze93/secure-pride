# Secure Pride — Do Not Use

> Version 1.0 | Vector System

These rules are non-negotiable. Every item below destroys the encoded meaning
of the identity system.

---

## Never Modify

### Abrasion

- Do not increase scratch density
- Do not add new scratch paths outside the `/abrasion-system` group
- Do not move scratches to upper shield or centered positions
- Do not make scratches symmetrical
- Do not animate or pulse scratches

### Prism

- Do not add new prism paths beyond the defined set
- Do not extend prism paths into a continuous arc across the shield
- Do not increase prism opacity above 15%
- Do not use full rainbow fills for prism strokes

### Light and Effects

- Do not add glow or bloom effects
- Do not add drop shadows
- Do not add outer glow
- Do not increase any opacity beyond spec values

### Geometry

- Do not re-center the left-biased asymmetry of the abrasion system
- Do not make the abrasion placement symmetrical
- Do not alter the heart shape
- Do not alter the shield proportions
- Do not add new decorative elements inside the shield

### Color

- Do not substitute the brass gradient colors
- Do not add new fills or gradient stops
- Do not use the prismatic colors at full opacity
- Do not use the prismatic gradient as a fill (strokes only)

---

## Never Scale Incorrectly

- Do not use the Tier 1 master at sizes below 512px
- Do not add abrasion to Tier 3 (32–64px) files
- Do not scale scratches by ratio — use the correct tier file

---

## Never Use

- On cluttered photographic backgrounds
- Stretched, skewed, or non-uniform scaled
- With the colors inverted in a way not specified by the mono system
- With any additional text overlaid on the shield body
- Rotated (the directional logic is vertical)

---

## Why These Rules Exist

The abrasion is directional. It implies real-world pressure on something built to
protect. If it becomes decorative or symmetrical, the meaning collapses.

The prism is restrained. At full opacity it becomes decoration. At its specified
opacity it is: light caught on specific damaged edges.

The asymmetry is intentional. Re-centering it removes the implication that the
mark has been used, worn, and survived contact with the world.

Form encodes philosophy. Violating the rules doesn't just change how it looks —
it changes what it means.
